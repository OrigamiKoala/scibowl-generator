# Competition Format

NSB is a 1 team on 1 team Quiz bowl style competition, but instead of all the standard quiz bowl topics, only covers the science topics: math, chemistry, physics, earth and space science, and energy (miscellaneous - can be any of the above). Each question comes from strictly one of those topics (e.g. they do not mix math and chemistry). 

In the actual competition, a moderator will read out the question to the two opposing teams, each player having their own buzzer. The players compete to answer the questions as fast as possible.

Each NSB packet has 23 sets of questions (known as tossup/bonus pairs). Each tossup/bonus pair is comprised of a tossup question that either team can answer. Once the moderator finishes reading the question, and no team has buzzed in the middle of the question, both teams have 5 seconds to answer. If one team gets the tossup right, the bonus question is read for them, and once the moderator finishes reading, that team has 20 seconds to answer. The opposing team cannot answer. 

Tossup questions are generally easier than bonus questions, and most of the time are qualitative, since the players have 5 seconds to answer. Bonus questions are mostly also qualitative, but in math and physics (and sometimes chemistry) are more often quantitative.

There are two types of questions - multiple choice and short answer. There are no free response/proof style questions.

Often, competitors will interrupt the moderator and attempt to predict what the question will ask before it is done so, and answer accordingly. Many format-match, meaning for multiple choice questions, the answer choices are often structured to aid pre-buzzing. 

# Subjects

There are 6 subjects: Math, Physics, Chemistry, Biology, Earth and Space Science, and Energy. Include an about equal amount of each.

# Difficulty Levels

National Science Bowl has three main difficulty levels: Regionals Round Robin (Rounds 1-5), Nationals Round Robin (Rounds 6-10), and Nationals Double Elimination (Rounds 11-16). Please see past_packets/ for a sense of how the rounds' difficulty levels are structured. Generally, tossups are easier than bonuses and do not involve calculations, while bonuses may.

# Packet Format

***CRITICAL*** Answer choices for multiple choice questions are labeled "W, X, Y, Z" instead of "A, B, C, D".

The packet groups each tossup-bonus pair together. First, it outlines which type of question it is (Tossup/Bonus + #, then subject, then Multiple Choice/Short Answer). Then, the question text is presented, followed by answer choices (if there are any). Each answer choice gets its own line. Finally, the Answer is added at the end of each question.
